Senate Unanimously Votes to Replace Filibuster With Competitive Eating Contest

In what both sides are calling "the most bipartisan thing to happen since the vending machine in the break room," legislators have agreed that political disagreements should be settled with hot dogs, not hot takes.

By CHAD THUNDERPEN April 13, 2026 | 5 min read
SHARE:

WASHINGTON, D.C. -- In a move that constitutional scholars are describing as "technically not illegal, but spiritually unhinged," the United States Senate voted 100-0 on Thursday to permanently replace the filibuster with a competitive eating contest. The resolution, officially titled the Bipartisan Resolution for Accelerated Tasteful Settlements (BRATS), will go into effect immediately. It is, by every measurable standard, the most productive thing Congress has done since naming a post office in 2019.

Under the new rules, any senator wishing to block legislation will no longer be required to stand and speak for hours on end. Instead, they must consume a minimum of 22 Nathan's Famous Hot Dogs -- buns mandatory -- within a 10-minute window. If they succeed, the bill is tabled. If they fail, or vomit, the bill passes automatically. Vomiting, the resolution specifies in a 14-page appendix titled "Regurgitation Protocols," constitutes "a withdrawal of objection and also a biohazard event."

"This is a historic day for democracy," said Senate Majority Leader Patricia Grindstone, wiping mustard from her blazer during the post-vote press conference. "For too long, we've asked Americans to watch elderly senators read 'Green Eggs and Ham' at 2 a.m. The people deserve better theater. They deserve competitive eating. They deserve to see a 73-year-old man from Nebraska cry into a pile of hot dog buns because he believes in fiscal responsibility."

"We've been talking in circles for decades. At least now we're eating in circles. This is progress. This is America."

The vote came after a six-month pilot program during which the Senate informally tested the system. According to internal records obtained by News Tabloid Now, the pilot period saw a 340% increase in legislative productivity, a 1,200% increase in antacid purchases at the Capitol Hill CVS, and exactly one incident in which a senator from Oregon attempted to substitute tofu dogs and was escorted from the chamber by the Sergeant at Arms.

A History of Doing Nothing, Deliciously

The traditional filibuster has long been criticized as an outdated mechanism that allows a single senator to hold up the entire legislative process by simply refusing to stop talking. Critics have noted that this is functionally identical to Thanksgiving dinner with extended family, except with worse food and higher stakes. Supporters of the filibuster have argued that it protects minority rights, though they have struggled to explain how reading a phone book into the congressional record for nine hours accomplishes this.

Senator Marcus Delray (R-TX), who originally proposed the resolution after watching a Joey Chestnut documentary on a cross-country flight, said the idea came to him "like a vision from the founding fathers, if the founding fathers had been at a state fair."

"I was watching this man consume 76 hot dogs in ten minutes, and I thought, that's more than Congress has accomplished in the last six years," Delray told reporters. "If one man can eat 76 hot dogs, surely 100 senators can eat their way to a functioning government. Also, I was three gin and tonics deep. I want to be transparent about that."

Delray then revealed that he had trained for the pilot program with a competitive eating sensei named "Thunderjaw Mike," a 280-pound man from Branson, Missouri, who holds the world record for consuming 114 corn dogs in a single sitting. "Mike taught me things about my esophagus that I did not want to know," Delray said, his eyes distant. "But I am a better senator for it."

Editor's Note: Senator Delray's office later clarified that he was watching the documentary on a taxpayer-funded iPad, and that "Thunderjaw Mike" is not a registered lobbyist, though he probably should be.

The Parliamentarian Quits, Un-Quits, Gets Hot Dogs

According to the 47-page rulebook drafted by the Senate Parliamentarian, the new system works as follows:

  • Any senator may invoke the "Gustatory Objection" to block a bill.
  • The objecting senator must then consume a minimum of 22 hot dogs (buns included) within 10 minutes.
  • If successful, the bill is tabled for 30 days, during which the senator is excused from all floor votes "to recover and reflect on what they have done."
  • If the senator fails, the bill proceeds to an immediate vote.
  • Condiments are permitted but not required. Ketchup remains a point of bipartisan contention that nearly derailed the entire resolution.
  • All contests are broadcast live on C-SPAN 3, which saw its viewership skyrocket from 12 viewers to 4.7 million overnight during the pilot program.

The Senate Parliamentarian, Elizabeth MacDougall, resigned in protest immediately after drafting the rulebook, calling it "the single most degrading document I have produced in 22 years of public service." She un-resigned four hours later after being offered free hot dogs for life and a reserved seat at the official scoring table. "I have reconsidered," MacDougall said in a statement. "The benefits package is competitive."

Nathan's Famous, the official hot dog supplier, has been granted a 10-year exclusive contract worth an undisclosed sum. A company spokesperson said they were "honored to serve democracy" and announced a new limited-edition product called the "Fili-Buster Dog" -- a foot-long frank wrapped in red, white, and blue casing, available at Senate gift shops and, inexplicably, Cracker Barrel.

The Hot Dog Lobby Enters the Chat

Perhaps the most alarming development to emerge from the new system is the sudden and aggressive rise of the National Hot Dog and Sausage Council, a lobbying organization that most Americans did not know existed until Thursday and now wish they still didn't.

The Council, which apparently has an annual budget of $4.1 million, donated $2.3 million to both parties within 72 hours of the resolution passing. "We are a nonpartisan organization," said Council president Janet Linkmeyer, from behind a podium shaped like a hot dog. "We believe in democracy. We believe in frankfurters. We believe they are the same thing."

"We believe in democracy. We believe in frankfurters. We believe they are the same thing." -- National Hot Dog and Sausage Council president

"The fact that a Hot Dog Lobby exists is troubling enough," said Dr. Harold Finch, Chair of the Department of Questionable Decisions at Yale. "The fact that it just became the most powerful lobbying group in Washington is the kind of sentence I never expected to say with a straight face, and yet here I am, saying it, with the straightest face I can manage."

Bipartisan Support, Bipartisan Indigestion

The vote marked a rare moment of unity in an otherwise deeply divided chamber. Senator Ellen Park (D-CA) called it "the first time I've agreed with my colleagues across the aisle on anything since we jointly decided the Senate cafeteria needs better coffee."

Not everyone was enthusiastic. Senator Margaret Collins (R-ME) approached the podium during the pilot program, looked at the pile of 22 hot dogs with undisguised contempt, and consumed exactly one (1) hot dog. She then dabbed her mouth with a cloth napkin, declared "This is beneath the dignity of this institution," stood in silence for approximately four seconds, and then ate six more in rapid succession. She declined to comment afterward, though her chief of staff confirmed that she "found them acceptable."

The American Medical Association issued a statement expressing "grave concern" about the health implications of the new rule, noting that the average senator is 64 years old and "not exactly in competitive eating shape." Dr. Priya Vasquez, a cardiologist at Johns Hopkins who was not consulted during the drafting of the resolution, ran what she called "a basic mortality simulation."

"We've run the numbers," Dr. Vasquez said, adjusting her glasses in a way that suggested the numbers were bad. "If every senator who currently threatens to filibuster actually has to eat 22 hot dogs, we're looking at a 400% increase in congressional hospital visits. The Capitol physician's office has already ordered six additional defibrillators, a stomach pump, and what I can only describe as an alarming quantity of Pepto-Bismol."

Public reaction has been overwhelmingly positive. A Gallup poll found that 87% of Americans approve of the new system, 9% are concerned about cholesterol, and exactly 4% believe this was already how Congress worked. The margin of error is plus or minus "who cares," according to pollsters.

Editor's Note: We reached out to several constitutional law professors for comment. Dr. Rita Huang of Georgetown University responded: "The founding fathers intended for the Senate to be a deliberative body. They did not intend for it to be a competitive eating venue. But I'll admit, the C-SPAN ratings are unprecedented, and Alexander Hamilton would have absolutely destroyed a pile of hot dogs. That man had the energy of a golden retriever."

C-SPAN Discovers What Ratings Are

C-SPAN has announced it will rebrand C-SPAN 3 as "C-SPAN Eats" and is in talks with Guy Fieri to provide color commentary. The network's ratings department, reached for comment, said simply: "We're finally going to need a ratings department. We literally did not have one. We had a guy named Phil who checked the website once a month."

During the pilot program, C-SPAN's viewership went from 12 people -- "most of whom we believe were watching by accident," a spokesperson admitted -- to 4.7 million for the final contest, in which Senator Grindstone successfully consumed 24 hot dogs to block an amendment about daylight saving time. The clip was viewed 89 million times on TikTok, where it was set to the audio from that one Drake song.

What Happens Next

The first official Gustatory Objection is expected as early as next week, when a controversial infrastructure bill reaches the Senate floor. Senator Tom Brackish (R-AL) has already announced his intention to invoke the rule, telling reporters he has been "training with Thunderjaw Mike" and that the experience has "changed him as a man and as a legislator, though mostly as a man."

Senator Brackish's physician has declined to comment. His wife has asked the media to "please stop calling."

Meanwhile, the House of Representatives is reportedly exploring its own version of the rule, though early proposals suggest replacing filibusters with a Spelling Bee, which insiders say would "eliminate roughly half the chamber within the first round."

In a development that has alarmed exactly everyone, the Supreme Court issued a brief statement late Friday indicating that it is "exploring similar reforms for oral arguments," though it declined to specify whether this meant competitive eating or simply the acknowledgment that most oral arguments could be resolved faster with condiments. Chief Justice Roberts was seen leaving a Nathan's Famous location in Georgetown carrying what witnesses described as "a suspicious amount of hot dogs for one man."
