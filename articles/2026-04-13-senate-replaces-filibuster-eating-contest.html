Senate Unanimously Votes to Replace Filibuster With Competitive Eating Contest

In what both sides are calling "the most bipartisan thing to happen since the vending machine in the break room," legislators have agreed that political disagreements should be settled with hot dogs, not hot takes.

By CHAD THUNDERPEN April 13, 2026 | 5 min read
SHARE:

WASHINGTON, D.C. - In a move that constitutional scholars are describing as "technically not illegal," the United States Senate voted 100-0 on Thursday to permanently replace the filibuster with a competitive eating contest. The resolution, officially titled the Bipartisan Resolution for Accelerated Tasteful Settlements (BRATS), will go into effect immediately.

Under the new rules, any senator wishing to block legislation will no longer be required to stand and speak for hours on end. Instead, they must consume an undisclosed quantity of Nathan's Famous Hot Dogs within a 10-minute window. If they succeed, the bill is tabled. If they fail, or vomit, the bill passes automatically.

"This is a historic day for democracy," said Senate Majority Leader Patricia Grindstone, wiping mustard from her blazer during the post-vote press conference. "For too long, we've asked Americans to watch elderly senators read 'Green Eggs and Ham' at 2 a.m. The people deserve better theater. They deserve competitive eating."

"We've been talking in circles for decades. At least now we're eating in circles."

The vote came after a six-month pilot program during which the Senate informally tested the system. According to internal records obtained by News Tabloid Now, the pilot period saw a 340% increase in legislative productivity and a 1,200% increase in antacid purchases at the Capitol Hill CVS.

A History of Doing Nothing, Deliciously

The traditional filibuster has long been criticized as an outdated mechanism that allows a single senator to hold up the entire legislative process by simply refusing to stop talking. Critics have noted that this is functionally identical to Thanksgiving dinner with extended family, except with worse food and higher stakes.

Senator Marcus Delray (R-TX), who originally proposed the resolution after watching a Joey Chestnut documentary on a cross-country flight, said the idea came to him "like a vision."

"I was watching this man consume 76 hot dogs in ten minutes, and I thought, that's more than Congress has accomplished in the last six years," Delray told reporters. "If one man can eat 76 hot dogs, surely 100 senators can eat their way to a functioning government."

Editor's Note: Senator Delray's office later clarified that he was watching the documentary on a taxpayer-funded iPad.

The Rules of Engagement

According to the 47-page rulebook drafted by the Senate Parliamentarian (who reportedly requested a transfer to the Library of Congress immediately afterward), the new system works as follows:

  • Any senator may invoke the "Gustatory Objection" to block a bill.
  • The objecting senator must then consume a minimum of 22 hot dogs (buns included) within 10 minutes.
  • If successful, the bill is tabled for 30 days, during which the senator is excused from all floor votes to recover.
  • If the senator fails, the bill proceeds to an immediate vote.
  • Condiments are permitted but not required. Ketchup remains a point of bipartisan contention.
  • All contests are broadcast live on C-SPAN 3, which is expected to see its first viewers since 2019.

Nathan's Famous, the official hot dog supplier, has been granted a 10-year exclusive contract worth an undisclosed sum. A company spokesperson said they were "honored to serve democracy" and announced a new limited-edition product called the "Fili-Buster Dog" -- a foot-long frank wrapped in red, white, and blue casing.

Bipartisan Support, Bipartisan Indigestion

The vote marked a rare moment of unity in an otherwise deeply divided chamber. Senator Ellen Park (D-CA) called it "the first time I've agreed with my colleagues across the aisle on anything since we jointly decided the Senate cafeteria needs better coffee."

Not everyone is celebrating. The American Medical Association issued a statement expressing "grave concern" about the health implications of the new rule, noting that the average senator is 64 years old and "not exactly in competitive eating shape."

"We've run the numbers," said Dr. Harold Finch, a cardiologist who was not consulted during the drafting of the resolution. "If every senator who currently threatens to filibuster actually has to eat 22 hot dogs, we're looking at a 400% increase in congressional hospital visits. The Capitol physician's office has already ordered six additional defibrillators."

"The founding fathers intended for the Senate to be a deliberative body. They did not intend for it to be a competitive eating venue. But I'll admit, this is more entertaining." -- Constitutional law professor Dr. Rita Huang, Georgetown University

Public reaction has been overwhelmingly positive. A poll conducted by News Tabloid Now found that 87% of Americans approve of the new system, 9% are concerned about cholesterol, and 4% believe this was already how Congress worked.

What Happens Next

The first official Gustatory Objection is expected as early as next week, when a controversial infrastructure bill reaches the Senate floor. Senator Tom Brackish (R-AL) has already announced his intention to invoke the rule, telling reporters he has been "training with a local competitive eater named 'The Gullet' and feels confident."

Senator Brackish's physician has declined to comment.

Meanwhile, the House of Representatives is reportedly exploring its own version of the rule, though early proposals suggest replacing filibusters with a Spelling Bee, which insiders say would "eliminate roughly half the chamber."
