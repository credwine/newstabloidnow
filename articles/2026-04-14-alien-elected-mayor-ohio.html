Alien Elected Mayor of Small Town in Ohio; Runs on Platform of 'Superior Infrastructure'

In a landslide victory that has baffled political analysts and thrilled pothole enthusiasts, the extraterrestrial candidate won 85% of the vote on a platform of molecular resurfacing, solar teleportation garbage disposal, and extended library hours.

By DIRK SCUTTLEBUTT April 14, 2026 | 6 min read
SHARE:

MILLFIELD, OHIO -- The residents of this small Athens County village (pop. 847) have elected a 7-foot grey alien as their new mayor, in what the Associated Press is calling "an event we are not entirely sure how to categorize." The candidate, who goes by Zylox-9 and lists his place of origin as "a region your cartography has not yet indexed," won 85% of the vote in a special election that saw the highest turnout in Millfield's 172-year history. Seven people who had not voted since 1986 showed up. Two of them brought lawn chairs.

Zylox-9 first appeared in Millfield approximately 14 months ago, descending from what witnesses described as "a beam of blue-white light and a faint smell of ozone" in the parking lot of the Millfield General Store. Harold Pembrook, the store's owner, was the first person to interact with the candidate. "He walked in, bought a Gatorade, paid in exact change, and asked where the potholes were," Pembrook recalled. "I figured he was from the county. It was dark. He was tall. We get tall people sometimes."

Within three weeks, Zylox-9 had personally inspected every road in Millfield, cataloging 347 potholes, 12 missing stop signs, and what he described in a formal report as "a drainage system that appears to have been designed by a species still in its cognitive infancy." The report was 90 pages long, written in flawless English, and included a section titled "Recommendations for a Civilization That Has Only Recently Discovered Concrete."

"I have traveled 4.7 billion light-years. Your roads are the worst thing I have encountered. This is not an exaggeration. I have seen black holes. Your roads are worse."

His decision to run for mayor, he explained at his campaign launch event -- held in the Millfield Community Center to a crowd of 214, which is 25% of the town -- was motivated by what he called "a moral obligation to infrastructure." His campaign speech, delivered in a flat, measured tone that one attendee described as "like a GPS that went to law school," outlined a three-point platform:

First, he would fix every road in Millfield using a process he called "molecular resurfacing," which he described as "rearranging the atomic structure of the existing road surface to eliminate all imperfections." When asked how this worked, he said, "I will show you. It is easier than explaining. Your language has limitations." When asked how much it would cost, he said, "Nothing. I have the equipment in my vehicle." He gestured toward the sky.

Second, he would reform the town's garbage collection by implementing what he called "solar teleportation," a process in which household waste would be transported directly into the sun. "It is a star," he explained to a room full of people who were clearly aware that the sun is a star. "It is already on fire. Your garbage will not make it more on fire. This is efficient." When a resident asked about recycling, Zylox-9 stared at her for approximately 11 seconds before saying, "Yes. We will also do that."

Third, he would extend library hours. This received the loudest applause of the evening, louder even than the molecular resurfacing announcement. Millfield's library currently closes at 4 PM on weekdays, a schedule that librarian Dorothy Finch has called "a crime against literacy." Zylox-9 proposed keeping it open until 9 PM and adding "a modest collection of interstellar literature, for those who are curious."

The Campaign Trail

Zylox-9 ran what political consultants are calling "the most disciplined grassroots campaign in Ohio history, and also the most confusing." He went door-to-door, which is standard for small-town politics. What was not standard was that he arrived at each door via a small hovering platform that emitted a low humming sound, and that his campaign literature was printed on a material that no one has been able to identify. "It felt like paper but also like water," said resident Marlene Tubbs. "I have been holding it for three days and I still do not understand what it is made of."

His yard signs glowed faintly in the dark, a feature that his campaign manager -- a 34-year-old Millfield native named Travis Dempsey who was previously the assistant manager at a Dollar General -- described as "just good branding." When pressed on how the signs glowed without batteries, electricity, or any visible power source, Dempsey said, "I asked him about that once. He said it was 'ambient photon recycling.' I nodded like I understood. I did not understand. I still do not understand."

"Honestly, I don't care if he's from space. Jim Henderson had 12 years to fix Elm Street. Twelve years. My grandmother fell in that pothole in 2019 and we had to get her out with a stepladder."

His opponent, incumbent Mayor Jim Henderson, ran on experience and familiarity, which proved insufficient against a candidate who could levitate. Henderson's concession speech was gracious, if somewhat bewildered. "I want to congratulate Mayor-elect Zylox-9 on a hard-fought campaign," Henderson said, reading from prepared remarks that his hands were visibly shaking while holding. "He ran on the issues. He connected with voters. He has a plan for the roads. I wish him well. I am going to go home now and think about some things."

Legal Challenges and Constitutional Questions

The Ohio State Board of Elections attempted to challenge Zylox-9's candidacy on citizenship grounds. The challenge was filed on a Tuesday, reviewed on a Wednesday, and abandoned on a Thursday after the Board's legal team determined that "the United States Constitution, the Ohio Revised Code, and approximately 240 years of electoral law do not contain a single provision addressing the eligibility of extraterrestrial candidates."

Board member Patricia Langley told reporters: "We looked. We looked very hard. The founding fathers did not anticipate this. Honestly, they barely anticipated Ohio." She added that the Board would "recommend the legislature address this gap at some point," but acknowledged that "they probably have other things going on."

The ACLU issued a statement supporting Zylox-9's right to run, calling it "a matter of fundamental fairness and also something we never thought we would weigh in on." The statement was three paragraphs long and included a footnote that read: "We acknowledge that this is unusual."

The Inauguration

Zylox-9's inauguration was held on the steps of the Millfield Municipal Building, a single-story structure that also houses the post office and a vending machine that has been broken since 2021. The ceremony was attended by all 847 residents of Millfield, plus an estimated 3,000 visitors from surrounding counties, 47 members of the national press corps, and a drone that no one claimed ownership of but that several Pentagon officials were later seen collecting from a nearby field.

The inauguration speech lasted three minutes and 12 seconds. It was delivered in a language that no linguist present could identify, though several described it as "tonal, efficient, and vaguely soothing, like a wind chime that has opinions." A real-time translation was provided on a holographic display that appeared to project from Zylox-9's left wrist. The translated text was largely incomprehensible, containing phrases such as "the substrate of civic obligation transcends molecular origin" and "your water table requires immediate attention."

However, the final line of the speech -- "Your roads will be adequate" -- was delivered in clear, unaccented English and received a standing ovation that lasted four minutes. Several people were openly weeping. Dorothy Finch, the librarian, was seen pumping her fist.

Federal Response

The Pentagon has issued a statement consisting entirely of the words "No comment," which it delivered through a spokesperson who appeared to be sweating more than the temperature warranted. However, residents have reported the installation of what appears to be surveillance equipment at the Millfield Dairy Queen, the Millfield General Store, and on a telephone pole outside the mayor's new office.

When asked about the equipment, a woman in a black SUV who declined to identify herself or her agency said, "What equipment?" She then drove away at a speed that seemed unnecessary for a residential street.

Travis Dempsey, the campaign manager, said he was not concerned. "If the federal government wants to watch Mayor Zylox-9 fix potholes, they are welcome to. It is going to be the most boring surveillance operation in history. He wakes up at 4 AM, reads municipal code for two hours, and then goes outside and stares at roads. That is his whole day. He does not even eat lunch. I have never seen him eat anything."

Resident Gladys Hornbuckle, 78, summarized the town's sentiment when she told this reporter: "My late husband always said, 'I don't care who fixes the roads, as long as someone fixes the roads.' Well, Gerald, someone is fixing the roads. He is from outer space and his name has a number in it. But he is fixing the roads. Rest in peace, you wonderful man."

As of press time, the pothole on Elm Street has been repaired. It took Zylox-9 approximately 45 seconds. He used a device that looked like a flashlight. The road is now smoother than it was when it was first paved in 1987. Marlene Tubbs drove over it six times in a row and called this reporter in tears.
