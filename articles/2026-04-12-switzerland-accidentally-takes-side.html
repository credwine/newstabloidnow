Switzerland Accidentally Takes a Side, Throws Entire Country Into Existential Crisis

What began as a minor clerical error at the United Nations has spiraled into the worst identity crisis in Swiss history, surpassing even the Great Fondue Schism of 1847.

By BLAINE DEADLINE April 12, 2026 | 6 min read
SHARE:

BERN, SWITZERLAND -- The Swiss Confederation, a nation that has built its entire national identity on the radical concept of not having an opinion about anything, is in chaos tonight after a mid-level government official accidentally liked a tweet. The tweet in question, posted by the UN General Assembly's official account, asked: "Does pineapple belong on pizza? Reply with your take!" The Swiss Federal Department of Foreign Affairs' verified account liked it at 2:47 p.m. Central European Time. By 2:48 p.m., the Swiss franc had dropped 12%. By 2:49 p.m., the Swiss Ambassador to the United Nations was on the phone with Bern, hyperventilating.

The like, which Swiss officials have described as "a catastrophic misclick of historic proportions," was performed by junior communications officer Markus Felder, 28, who told investigators he was "scrolling during lunch" and "thought he was on his personal account." He was not on his personal account. He was logged into the official diplomatic account of a nation that has maintained strict neutrality since 1815, a 211-year streak that was broken by a single thumb on a single screen over a question about tropical fruit on bread with cheese.

"I want to be absolutely clear," Swiss President Viola Amherd said in an emergency nationally televised address, gripping the podium with both hands. "The Swiss Confederation does not have an opinion on whether pineapple belongs on pizza. We do not have an opinion on pizza. We do not have an opinion on pineapple. We do not have an opinion on belonging. That is our entire national philosophy. One man with a phone has jeopardized two centuries of principled indifference."

The Ambassador Makes It Worse

In what diplomats are calling "the most consequential social media error since that time the French embassy accidentally posted a picture of a croissant on the German embassy's anniversary," the situation escalated dramatically when Swiss Ambassador to the UN Heinrich Broder attempted to unlike the tweet.

He retweeted it instead.

Ambassador Broder, a 62-year-old career diplomat who has described social media as "a carnival for the emotionally unregulated," then attempted to delete the retweet, accidentally quoted it with the text "jjjkk" (what appears to be a pocket tweet), deleted that, and in the process somehow followed three accounts: the official Domino's Pizza account, a Hawaiian tourism board, and, inexplicably, Guy Fieri.

"The Swiss Confederation does not have an opinion on pineapple. We do not have an opinion on pizza. We do not have an opinion on belonging. One man with a phone has jeopardized two centuries of principled indifference."

"It was the worst 90 seconds of my diplomatic career," Broder said in a statement released through his office, which was visibly typed in haste, as it contained two typos and a sentence that simply read: "We are neutral. We are neutral. We are neutral." He has since deactivated the account entirely, though not before Domino's replied with "Welcome to the family" and a pizza emoji, a response that Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Renaldi called "an act of war."

Markets React to Switzerland Having an Opinion About Toppings

The Swiss franc dropped 12% within minutes of the like becoming public, as global markets reacted to what analysts are calling "an unprecedented expression of preference from the world's most aggressively neutral country."

"The Swiss franc is fundamentally a neutrality-backed currency," explained Dr. Helga Braun, chief economist at Deutsche Bank and Chair of the Institute for Things That Shouldn't Affect Markets But Do. "When Switzerland takes a side on anything -- even pizza toppings -- it undermines the entire basis of the currency. Investors are asking: if Switzerland can have an opinion about pineapple, what's next? Fiscal policy? Climate? Whether Die Hard is a Christmas movie?"

The franc recovered briefly at 3:15 p.m., then dropped again at 3:22 p.m. when a reporter at the press conference asked President Amherd directly: "But do you like pineapple on pizza?" Amherd stood in silence for 19 seconds -- the longest recorded silence in a Swiss presidential address since the Great Fondue Schism of 1847 -- and then said, "Next question." The franc dropped another 4%.

The Zurich Stock Exchange dropped 8% before trading was halted. Credit Suisse's successor institution, Credit Suisse Again (they rebranded after the collapse, then collapsed again, then rebranded again), issued an emergency note to clients advising them to "remain calm and not form any opinions about anything until further notice."

Editor's Note: When reached for comment, a Zurich-based hedge fund manager said, "I have traded through three financial crises, a pandemic, and a literal war. Nothing has rattled me like Switzerland having an opinion about pizza." He then asked us not to use his name because he didn't want to "take a side on whether to be quoted."

Italy Recalls Its Ambassador. Canada Does Too, But Nobody Notices.

International reaction has been swift, disproportionate, and in several cases, deeply personal.

Italy recalled its ambassador to Switzerland within three hours, with Italian Foreign Minister Marco Benedetti issuing a statement calling the incident "an affront to Italian culinary heritage, an act of gastronomic imperialism, and a violation of the spiritual contract between humanity and pizza." He then proposed a UN resolution condemning pineapple on pizza, which passed 94-12 with 47 abstentions. Switzerland abstained. Markets briefly stabilized.

Canada also recalled its ambassador, citing "solidarity with the pineapple community." However, no one noticed for approximately 36 hours, at which point a Canadian reporter asked the Swiss Foreign Ministry about it and was told, "We were not aware Canada had an ambassador here. We mean that respectfully. We are sure he is very nice."

France offered to mediate, which France always does, regardless of the conflict, the stakes, or whether anyone asked. "France has opinions about everything and is very experienced in having them," said French President Aurore Marchand. "We will coach Switzerland through this. It will be painful. Having opinions always is. But it builds character. Look at us. We have so much character it is exhausting."

The United States issued a brief statement through the State Department: "We welcome Switzerland to the world of having takes. It's terrible here. You'll hate it. Also, pineapple absolutely belongs on pizza. This is the official position of the United States government. We will not be taking questions."

"Neutrality is not a policy. It's a lifestyle. It's a religion. And today, we committed the ultimate heresy: we expressed a preference about a topping." -- Dr. Heinz Guttenberg, Professor of Non-Alignment Studies, University of Bern

Switzerland Attempts "Aggressive Neutrality," Confuses Everyone

In an effort to contain the crisis, the Swiss Federal Council convened an emergency session and attempted to issue a formal statement of "aggressive neutrality" -- a phrase that constitutional scholars immediately called "an oxymoron and also hilarious."

"You cannot be aggressively neutral," said Dr. Frieda Lenz, Professor of Constitutional Paradoxes at the University of Geneva. "That's like being passionately indifferent. Or violently calm. It is a contradiction in terms. But I will say this: only Switzerland would try. And honestly? I respect it. I respect the attempt. I don't have an opinion about whether it will work. I am Swiss. I am not allowed to have opinions right now."

The statement, which went through 14 drafts over six hours, ultimately read: "The Swiss Confederation neither endorses nor condemns pineapple on pizza. The Confederation acknowledges that pineapple exists, that pizza exists, and that both are foods. Beyond this, the Confederation has no position. The Confederation would like everyone to stop asking." It was released at 11:47 p.m. and immediately went viral, which is the exact opposite of what Switzerland wanted.

Swiss Chocolate Sales: A Study in Petty Geopolitics

The economic fallout has been both measurable and absurd. Swiss chocolate sales dropped 8% in Italy, where consumers organized a boycott under the hashtag #NoFormaggio (which means "no cheese" and has nothing to do with chocolate but was the only Italian food hashtag available at the time). Meanwhile, Swiss chocolate sales rose 23% in Hawaii, where residents interpreted the like as "Switzerland endorsing our lifestyle" and purchased Toblerone in solidarity.

"We did not endorse Hawaii's lifestyle," a spokesperson for the Swiss Department of Economic Affairs clarified. "We did not endorse any lifestyle. Switzerland does not endorse lifestyles. Please stop buying our chocolate for political reasons. Buy it because it is delicious. Actually, don't buy it for any reason. We don't care. We are neutral about our own chocolate."

On the streets of Zurich, Bern, and Geneva, the mood is one of quiet devastation. "My grandmother called me in tears," said Lukas Moser, 34, a software engineer in Zurich. "She said, 'We are a country that watches. We observe. We hold gold. We do not choose sides about pizza toppings.' I tried to comfort her, but she asked me if I thought pineapple belonged on pizza, and I panicked and hung up. I have not called her back. I don't know what to say. I am afraid she will ask again."

Swatch announced a limited-edition "Neutrality Watch" that displays no time, only the word "Perhaps." Rolex released a statement saying it "neither confirms nor denies the existence of pineapple."

The Department of Not Having Opinions

As of press time, the official who started it all -- junior communications officer Markus Felder -- has been reassigned to a newly created government body called "The Department of Not Having Opinions," which Switzerland had to invent specifically for this incident. The department, which has an annual budget of 2.4 million Swiss francs and a staff of one, is located in a windowless office in the basement of the Federal Palace. Its mandate, according to the official charter, is "to ensure that no representative of the Swiss Confederation expresses a preference, opinion, take, hot take, lukewarm take, subtweet, like, retweet, or emoji reaction on any platform, about any topic, ever, for any reason, including this charter."

Felder, reached for comment through his attorney, said: "I just liked a tweet about pizza. I like pizza. Everyone likes pizza. I didn't think -- " His attorney then physically covered his mouth and said, "My client has no further opinions."

The Swiss franc has recovered slightly, trading at only 9% below its pre-opinion value. Economists expect it to fully recover once Switzerland successfully completes a full calendar year without expressing a thought. Given that the nation is now 211 years into its neutrality streak and has to start over from zero because of a pizza tweet, analysts estimate full recovery will take until approximately 2227, "assuming no one in the Swiss government discovers TikTok."
