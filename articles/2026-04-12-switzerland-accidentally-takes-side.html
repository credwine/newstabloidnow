Switzerland Accidentally Takes a Side, Throws Entire Country Into Existential Crisis

What began as a minor clerical error at the United Nations has spiraled into the worst identity crisis in Swiss history, surpassing even the Great Fondue Schism of 1847.

By BLAINE DEADLINE April 12, 2026 | 6 min read
SHARE:

BERN, SWITZERLAND - The Swiss Confederation, a nation that has built its entire national identity on the radical concept of not having an opinion about anything, is in chaos tonight after its delegate to the United Nations accidentally voted "yes" on a non-binding resolution about whether Tuesdays are better than Wednesdays.

The vote, which took place during a routine session of the UN General Assembly's Subcommittee on Calendar Optimization, was supposed to be an abstention. Switzerland has abstained from every vote in the subcommittee's 14-year history, including a vote on whether the subcommittee should continue to exist.

But on Thursday afternoon, Swiss delegate Markus Felder, 58, pressed the wrong button. He voted yes. Switzerland had, for the first time in modern recorded history, expressed a preference.

"I meant to hit 'Abstain,'" Felder told reporters, visibly shaken and holding what appeared to be his third espresso of the hour. "The buttons are right next to each other. This is not a reflection of Swiss foreign policy. Switzerland does not believe Tuesdays are better than Wednesdays. Switzerland does not believe anything is better than anything. That is our whole thing."

Markets React to Switzerland Having a Thought

The Swiss franc dropped 12% within minutes of the vote becoming public, as global markets reacted to what analysts are calling "an unprecedented expression of preference from the world's most aggressively neutral country."

"The Swiss franc is fundamentally a neutrality-backed currency," explained Helga Braun, chief economist at Deutsche Bank. "When Switzerland takes a side on anything -- even the relative merits of weekdays -- it undermines the entire basis of the currency. Investors are asking: if Switzerland can have an opinion about Tuesdays, what's next? Fiscal policy?"

The Zurich Stock Exchange dropped 8% before trading was halted. Credit Suisse's successor institution, Credit Suisse Again (they rebranded), issued an emergency note to clients advising them to "remain calm and not form any opinions until further notice."

The Political Fallout

Swiss President Viola Amherd called an emergency session of the Federal Council within an hour of the vote, marking the first time the council has convened on short notice since 2003, when someone accidentally put milk chocolate in the dark chocolate section of the parliamentary vending machine.

"On behalf of the Swiss Confederation, I wish to formally apologize to the international community for having a thought," Amherd said in a nationally televised address. "This does not represent who we are. We are a people who pride ourselves on having no position, no stance, and no strong feelings about anything, including this apology."

The president went on to announce a three-point plan:

  1. Delegate Felder has been placed on administrative leave and is undergoing "neutrality rehabilitation" at an undisclosed retreat in the Alps, where he will be asked to meditate on fence-sitting for 14 days.
  2. All UN voting terminals assigned to Swiss delegates will be retrofitted with a protective cover over the "Yes" and "No" buttons, leaving only "Abstain" accessible without a key.
  3. A national day of non-opinion has been declared for next Friday, during which Swiss citizens are encouraged to not express a preference about anything, including breakfast.
Editor's Note: When asked whether Friday was the right day for the national day of non-opinion, President Amherd paused for 11 seconds and then said, "It is a day. We acknowledge that it is a day." She declined to elaborate.

Citizens in Crisis

On the streets of Zurich, Bern, and Geneva, the mood is one of quiet devastation. Several citizens reported feeling "profoundly disoriented" by the idea that their country might have a position on something.

"My grandmother called me in tears," said Lukas Moser, 34, a software engineer in Zurich. "She said, 'We are a country that watches. We observe. We hold gold. We do not choose sides about Tuesdays.' I tried to comfort her, but she asked me if I thought Tuesdays were better than Wednesdays, and I panicked and hung up."

In Geneva, a city that hosts more international organizations per capita than any other and yet somehow has no opinion about any of them, a small protest formed outside the Palais des Nations. Approximately 200 people gathered, holding signs that read "We Stand For Nothing" and "Neutrality or Neutrality." When asked what they were protesting, several participants said they weren't sure but felt it was important to be there without taking a position.

The country's famous watchmakers have responded with particular alarm. Swatch announced a limited-edition "Neutrality Watch" that displays no time, only the word "Perhaps." Rolex released a statement saying it "neither confirms nor denies the existence of Tuesdays."

International Reaction

World leaders have responded with a mixture of shock, amusement, and, in some cases, concern.

French President Aurore Marchand said she was "deeply moved that Switzerland has finally experienced the sensation of having an opinion" and offered to "coach them through it, as France has opinions about everything and is very experienced in this area."

The United States issued a brief statement through the State Department: "We welcome Switzerland to the world of having takes. It's terrible here. You'll hate it."

Russia called the vote "a provocation." China called it "an internal Swiss matter." Canada said it was "sorry to hear about it" and then apologized for commenting.

"Neutrality is not a policy. It's a lifestyle. And today, we relapsed." -- Dr. Heinz Guttenberg, Professor of Non-Alignment Studies, University of Bern

As of press time, Delegate Felder remains in neutrality rehabilitation. His family has asked for privacy, noting that he has not expressed a preference about anything in the last 48 hours and is "making good progress." The Tuesday vs. Wednesday vote has been expunged from the UN record at Switzerland's request, though the damage, analysts say, may take years to undo.

The Swiss franc has recovered slightly, trading at only 9% below its pre-opinion value. Economists expect it to fully recover once Switzerland successfully completes a full calendar year without expressing a thought.
