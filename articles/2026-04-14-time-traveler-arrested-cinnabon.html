Time Traveler From 2847 Arrested at Mall Food Court; Says He 'Came Back for Cinnabon'

The man, who identified himself only as "Kevin," appeared in a flash of blue light at 2:14 PM on a Tuesday and proceeded to order 14 cinnamon rolls while weeping openly. Scientists cannot explain his watch. The DA cannot determine his crime.

By TANYA BLITZ April 14, 2026 | 5 min read
SHARE:

INDIANAPOLIS -- A man claiming to be a time traveler from the year 2847 was arrested Tuesday afternoon at the Westgate Mall food court after causing what police described as "a disturbance that we are not sure how to classify, legally or otherwise." The man, who would identify himself only as "Kevin" and refused to provide a last name on the grounds that "surnames were abolished in 2412, keep up," appeared in the food court at exactly 2:14 PM in a flash of blue light that temporarily knocked out the mall's Wi-Fi and caused a nearby Auntie Anne's pretzel warmer to emit a sound it had never made before.

Seventeen witnesses confirmed the flash of light. Mall security footage, which has since been turned over to federal authorities and also posted to TikTok by a teenager named Jayden, shows an empty space near the Orange Julius at 2:13 PM and a fully formed man standing in it at 2:14 PM. He is wearing what appears to be a full-body silver jumpsuit with no visible zippers, buttons, or seams. He is also wearing socks with sandals, which he later explained is "the dominant fashion paradigm of the 29th century" and "you people are the weird ones for not doing this."

Upon materializing, Kevin stood motionless for approximately four seconds, scanning the food court with an expression that witnesses described as "the face of a man seeing his dead dog alive again." His eyes locked onto the Cinnabon counter. He made a sound. Multiple witnesses described this sound differently -- "a gasp," "a whimper," "the noise my dad made when the Packers won the NFC Championship" -- but all agreed it was the sound of a man experiencing something profound.

"He just stood there, looking at the Cinnabon sign, and tears were streaming down his face. I thought he was having a medical episode. Then he whispered, 'It's real. It's still here. Oh God, it's still here,' and I realized this was something else entirely."

He approached the counter and ordered 14 Classic Cinnabon rolls. The employee, 19-year-old Madison Pruitt, told this reporter she initially assumed he was "doing a bit" but became concerned when he began crying into his second cinnamon roll. "He kept saying, 'You don't understand what you have. You don't understand what you're going to lose.' I offered him a napkin. He said napkins were also gone in the future. He cried harder."

When asked to pay, Kevin produced what he described as a "universal credit chit" -- a small, translucent cube approximately one inch in diameter that emitted a faint blue glow. The Cinnabon register did not accept it. Kevin seemed genuinely confused by this. "He kept tapping it on the counter like it was a credit card," Pruitt said. "Then he held it up to the screen. Then he said something like 'interface, authorize, Cinnabon' really slowly, like he was talking to a voice assistant. Nothing happened. He looked betrayed."

It was at this point that mall security was called. Officer Rick Darnell, a 12-year veteran of Westgate Mall security, arrived to find Kevin sitting on the floor surrounded by cinnamon rolls, several of which he had already eaten, telling nearby shoppers to "cherish this place" and "tell your children about Cinnabon." "In my 12 years of mall security, I have never encountered anything like this," Darnell told reporters. "And I once had to deal with a man who tried to live inside a Build-A-Bear. This was weirder."

The Interrogation

Kevin was transported to the Indianapolis Metropolitan Police Department's East District station, where he was questioned for approximately three hours. According to the police report, which this publication obtained through a public records request that the department seemed reluctant but legally obligated to fulfill, Kevin was cooperative, polite, and "appeared to be telling what he genuinely believed to be the truth, which made the whole thing significantly more unsettling."

During questioning, Kevin explained that he had traveled 821 years into the past for one reason and one reason only: Cinnabon. "In my time, Cinnabon closed in 2154," he said, according to the transcript. "It is considered the single greatest cultural loss in human history. There are memorials. There is a holiday. November 14th. National Cinnabon Remembrance Day. It is a solemn occasion. Schools are closed."

When detectives pressed him for information about the future -- wars, disasters, technological breakthroughs -- Kevin became evasive. "I am here for the pastries, not to alter the timeline," he said. "I am not an idiot. I took the temporal ethics course. Three times, actually, because I failed it twice, but the point stands."

He did, however, volunteer several pieces of information that he described as "harmless timeline-safe trivia":

  • The Cincinnati Reds win the 2089 World Series.
  • President Dwayne "The Rock" Johnson's second term is "widely regarded as the best."
  • Wi-Fi eventually becomes sentient and starts charging rent.
  • The state of Delaware is "still there, but nobody can explain why."
  • Cats learn to open doors in 2091. "It changes everything," Kevin said gravely.
"I failed temporal ethics twice. But the core principle is simple: don't mess with the big stuff. Wars, elections, plagues -- hands off. But cinnamon rolls? Cinnamon rolls are not load-bearing to the timeline. I checked."

The Watch

Upon his arrest, Kevin's personal effects were cataloged by police. These included the silver jumpsuit (which forensic analysis determined is "made of a fiber that does not correspond to any known textile"), the glowing credit cube, a small leather-bound notebook containing what appears to be a handwritten list of "every food that no longer exists in 2847" (Cinnabon is number one; number two is Arby's; number three, underlined three times, is "that specific type of gas station hot dog that has been on the roller for too long"), and a wristwatch.

The watch is a modified Casio F-91W, a $15 digital watch that has been in production since 1989. However, the interior of the watch, examined by a team of scientists from MIT who arrived in Indianapolis within six hours of Kevin's arrest with an urgency that several observers found "suspicious," contains components that do not match any known technology. "The circuit board, if you can call it that, appears to be made of a crystalline material that we have never seen before," said Dr. Alan Chowdhury, who led the MIT team. "It was also warm. Not battery-warm. Warm like it was alive. I do not have an explanation for that and I would prefer not to speculate."

When asked why his time travel device was a modified Casio watch, Kevin said: "The F-91W is the most reliable piece of technology humanity has ever produced. It has survived 800 years. Literally nothing else from your era has. Not your phones. Not your cars. Not your governments. The Casio endures. We built the temporal array into it because it is the only thing we trust."

Legal Proceedings

Kevin was arraigned Wednesday morning before Judge Patricia Colvin, who admitted from the bench that she was "at a complete loss." The prosecution, led by Assistant District Attorney Michael Frye, charged Kevin with disorderly conduct, trespassing, and theft of services (the 14 unpaid cinnamon rolls). Frye acknowledged that the charges were "the best we could come up with on short notice" and that there is "no statute in the state of Indiana addressing arrival from the future."

Kevin's public defender, a visibly overwhelmed man named Stuart Lipman, argued that his client could not be charged with trespassing because "he did not enter the mall through any door or entrance, and therefore technically never trespassed on anything. He simply appeared." Judge Colvin stared at Lipman for a very long time before saying, "I am going to need a recess."

Kevin was ultimately released on $500 bail, which was paid by a GoFundMe that reached $47,000 within four hours. The excess funds, per the organizer's description, will be donated to "Cinnabon preservation efforts." Cinnabon's corporate office has not commented on this.

As he left the courthouse, Kevin was mobbed by reporters. He answered no questions about the future, the timeline, or his technology. His only statement, delivered with the solemnity of a man who had seen things no one else would ever see, was: "Invest in soybeans. You're welcome."

He then walked behind a parked van and, according to four separate witnesses and one very confused pigeon, disappeared in a flash of blue light. The van's alarm went off. The pigeon has not been seen since.

The Reds' front office declined to comment but did say they "would not rule anything out at this point."
