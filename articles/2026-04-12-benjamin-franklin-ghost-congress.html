Ghost of Benjamin Franklin Seen Haunting Congress; Demands 'What Have You People Done'

Capitol security footage shows a translucent figure matching the description of Benjamin Franklin floating through the halls of Congress, attending a committee hearing, and leaving a handwritten note expressing "profound disappointment." Historians say the level of frustration is "historically consistent."

By DIRK SCUTTLEBUTT April 12, 2026 | 5 min read
SHARE:

WASHINGTON, D.C. -- The ghost of Benjamin Franklin haunted the United States Capitol for three consecutive days last week, attending committee hearings, wandering hallways, and expressing what multiple witnesses described as "a level of disappointment that felt personal and deeply earned." The apparition, first spotted by Capitol Police Officer Denise Yamamoto at 3:47 AM on a Tuesday near the House chamber vending machines, was identified by "the bifocals, the colonial attire, and the general aura of disappointment that could only belong to a man who helped invent the country and is now watching what happened to it."

Officer Yamamoto, a 14-year veteran of the Capitol Police force, filed an incident report that has since been obtained by this publication. The report, which she has described as "the most difficult document I have ever written, and I have written reports about congressmen getting stuck in elevators," reads in part: "At approximately 0347 hours, I observed a translucent male figure, approximately 5 feet 9 inches, heavyset, wearing period-appropriate colonial attire including bifocal spectacles and a fur-collared coat. The figure was hovering approximately six inches above the floor near vending machine C-7. The figure appeared to be reading the nutritional information on a bag of Sun Chips. It then shook its head slowly and sighed. The sigh was audible."

Yamamoto approached the figure and asked it to identify itself. According to her report, the figure turned, adjusted its spectacles, and said: "I am Benjamin Franklin. I helped write the Constitution. I invented the lightning rod. I founded the postal service. And I am here because I need to see for myself what you people have done, because the accounts I have been receiving in the afterlife are, frankly, upsetting."

"I left specific instructions. I wrote them down. I used excellent penmanship. I used my best quill. And yet, here we are."

Yamamoto did not detain the figure, noting in her report that "it is unclear what jurisdiction applies to deceased founding fathers" and that "my training did not cover this scenario." She did, however, offer him a Sun Chips. He declined, saying, "I do not know what those are, but I can see through my own hand, so I suspect chewing would be a challenge."

Day One: The Wandering

Franklin's ghost spent most of Tuesday wandering the halls of Congress, pausing at doorways to shake his head and, according to at least four separate witnesses, "audibly sighing." The sighing was described by multiple sources as "heavy, sustained, and laden with the specific exhaustion of a man who founded something important and has been forced to watch it from a great distance for 236 years."

He was spotted in the Rotunda, where he stood beneath the painting of the Declaration of Independence signing for approximately 20 minutes, looking up at it with an expression that one aide described as "the face my dad makes when he sees my credit card statement." He then turned to a passing intern and said: "I was there, you know. It was hot. The room smelled terrible. We were wearing wool. And we did it anyway, because we believed in something. I am beginning to wonder what."

The intern, a 22-year-old political science major from the University of Virginia named Marcus Webb, told reporters he initially thought it was a "very committed re-enactor." "Then he floated through a wall," Webb said. "Re-enactors don't do that. I checked."

Franklin was also seen in the congressional cafeteria at approximately 12:30 PM, hovering near the salad bar. Multiple witnesses reported that he picked up a menu, read the prices, and stared at a $14 salad "with visible contempt." A staffer heard him say: "Fourteen dollars for lettuce. I helped found this nation and you are charging fourteen dollars for lettuce. I once negotiated a treaty with France. The entire treaty cost less than this salad."

"I invented the lightning rod. I founded the postal service. I helped write the Constitution. And you people can't agree on a road. A road. I built roads. In the 1700s. With horses."

Day Two: The Committee Hearing

On Wednesday, Franklin's ghost attended a three-hour House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure hearing on federal highway funding. He was seen sitting -- or, more accurately, hovering approximately two inches above -- an empty chair in the public gallery. He remained for the entire three hours. Multiple witnesses confirmed that he spent the entirety of the hearing pinching the bridge of his nose.

At one point, when a representative from Ohio began a 12-minute speech about the importance of "bipartisan cooperation on road repair," Franklin was observed placing his face in his translucent hands and whispering what a nearby aide heard as: "Roads. They are fighting about roads. I built roads. In the 1700s. With horses. These people have machines. Machines that cost millions of dollars. And they cannot agree on a road."

The hearing took an unexpected turn when Committee Chairman Robert Dandridge (R-GA) noticed the apparition and, in a moment that C-SPAN footage confirms was not scripted, said: "I believe we have a distinguished visitor in the gallery. Sir, do you have any comment on the state of federal infrastructure?"

Franklin rose from his chair -- or, more accurately, rose further into the air -- and spoke. According to the Congressional Record, which has officially transcribed the exchange despite what the Parliamentarian described as "significant reservations," Franklin said: "I invented the lightning rod. I founded the postal service. I helped write the Constitution. And you people can't agree on a road. A road. Not a treaty. Not a declaration of independence. A road. I am going to be transparent with you: I am profoundly disappointed."

The chamber was silent for approximately 15 seconds. Then Representative Maria Fuentes (D-NM) began a slow clap, which spread through the gallery and lasted approximately two minutes. Franklin did not acknowledge the applause. He sat back down, folded his translucent arms, and resumed pinching the bridge of his nose.

Day Two: The Medium

Later that evening, Congressional leadership brought in a professional medium, Sandra Delacroix of Falls Church, Virginia, to "facilitate communication" with the apparition. Delacroix, who charges $350 per hour and has a 4.8-star rating on Yelp, set up in the Speaker's conference room with candles, crystals, and what she described as "a general vibe of receptivity."

Franklin appeared within four minutes of the session beginning, which Delacroix said was "the fastest response time I have ever experienced, and I once channeled a Civil War general who had been waiting 160 years to complain about his commanding officer."

Through Delacroix, Franklin delivered the following message: "Tell them to read the Federalist Papers. The WHOLE thing. Not just the parts they like. Not just the parts that support whatever they already believe. The whole thing. I wrote several of those. Well, Hamilton wrote most of them. But I edited. I was a printer. I know editing. And tell them to stop naming things after me if they're not going to read what I wrote. I have seen the buildings. I have seen the hundred-dollar bill. I do not need more buildings. I need them to read."

When Delacroix relayed this message to the assembled congressional aides, one of them asked if Franklin had any specific policy recommendations. Franklin, through Delacroix, responded: "Fix the roads. Expand the postal service. And for the love of God, stop arguing about things I settled in 1787. We had the argument. We wrote it down. It is in the document. Read the document."

Day Three: The Departure

Franklin's ghost was seen for the final time on Thursday afternoon, drifting through the Capitol's east corridor with what observers described as "the energy of a man who has seen enough." He paused briefly at a portrait of himself hanging near the Old Senate Chamber, regarded it for approximately 30 seconds, and said -- to no one in particular, as the hallway was empty except for a custodian named Wes who was "just trying to mop" -- "I looked better in person. The artist was generous with the hair. I appreciate the gesture."

He then drifted toward the Speaker's office, where he remained for approximately seven minutes. When the Speaker's chief of staff entered the office the next morning, she found a note on the Speaker's desk, written in handwriting that a forensic document examiner later confirmed is "consistent with known samples of Benjamin Franklin's penmanship, which is either remarkable evidence of paranormal activity or the most elaborate prank in American history."

The note read, in its entirety:

"I am profoundly disappointed but not surprised. I helped create a nation built on reason, compromise, and the radical idea that people could govern themselves. I see that the experiment continues. I see that it is messy. It was messy in my time, too. But we tried. I would like you to try harder. I would also like you to fix the vending machine on the second floor. It ate my quarter. I know I am a ghost and I do not need quarters, but it is the principle of the thing. -- B. Franklin"

The vending machine was repaired on Friday. It was the fastest infrastructure fix in Congress in recent memory.

Since Franklin's departure, three other founding fathers have been reported in the Capitol area, though none has been confirmed. A security guard on the overnight shift reported seeing "a very tall, very angry figure in a powdered wig" near the Senate chamber, but could not confirm if it was George Washington or "just a very tall, very angry person in a powdered wig, which, in Washington D.C., is not impossible."

Representative Fuentes has introduced a resolution to officially acknowledge Franklin's visit and "implement his recommendations, beginning with the vending machine and working up from there." The resolution currently has 14 co-sponsors, which Fuentes called "more than I expected and fewer than it deserves."

[Editor's Note]: We contacted several historians to verify the ghost's identity. Dr. Linda Vasquez of Georgetown University said, "I can't confirm it's Franklin, but the level of disappointment described is historically consistent. Franklin was a deeply pragmatic man who believed in functional government and good infrastructure. The fact that his ghost is upset about potholes and vending machines is, honestly, exactly what I would expect." Dr. Harold Finch of Yale added: "If any founding father was going to come back and yell at Congress, it would be Franklin. The man had opinions about everything. Everything. He once wrote a 12-page essay about the ideal design of a stove. He would absolutely have opinions about a $14 salad."
