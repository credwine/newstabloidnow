Local Influencer Hospitalized After Attempting to Live an Unfiltered Life for 24 Hours

What began as a wellness experiment quickly became a medical emergency when Brexley Waverly, 26, encountered the real world for the first time in four years.

By PEPPER SCOOP April 12, 2026 | 3 min read
SHARE:

LOS ANGELES -- Lifestyle influencer Brexley Waverly, known to her 2.3 million Instagram followers as @BrexleyBeams, was rushed to Cedars-Sinai Medical Center late Thursday evening after attempting to live for 24 consecutive hours without applying a single filter to any aspect of her existence. Doctors have created an entirely new diagnosis for her condition: Catastrophic Authenticity Disorder (CAD), which the attending physician described as "what happens when a person who has not experienced unmediated reality since 2022 suddenly encounters all of it at once, like walking into the sun after four years in a ring-lit cave."

The experiment, which Waverly had promoted on her channels as "The Raw Challenge," was intended to demonstrate her "authenticity" and "connection to the real." She announced the challenge to her followers on Wednesday night with a 47-slide carousel post and a 22-minute YouTube pre-video, both of which were heavily filtered and professionally lit. The announcement video alone used 14 different filters, three of which have not been released to the public and were, according to sources, developed by a former NASA engineer Waverly keeps on retainer.

"I want to show you guys the REAL me," Waverly said in the announcement video, which featured studio-grade color grading, a $400 ring light, and a barely visible teleprompter. "No filters. No edits. Just me. Raw. Uncut. Starting tomorrow at 8 a.m., or whenever I wake up naturally, which is also 8 a.m. because I set an alarm. Authenticity is my brand. I am so real it hurts."

It would, in fact, hurt.

The Unraveling: A Timeline of Catastrophic Authenticity

According to her manager, Derek Funnel, the first few hours went smoothly. Waverly ate breakfast without photographing it. She reported feeling "a strange sensation" that Funnel later identified as "eating food while it was still warm." She consumed an entire avocado toast without arranging the microgreens into a spiral, an act that witnesses described as "brave" and that her nutritionist described as "the same thing everyone does, just without the performance."

But by mid-morning, things began to deteriorate rapidly. At 9:47 a.m., Waverly attempted to eat a bowl of acai without arranging it in a mandala pattern and experienced what doctors would later describe as "a full existential episode." She stared at the unadorned bowl of purple mush for eleven minutes, whispered "this means nothing," and had to be talked down by her assistant, who reminded her that food can, in fact, be consumed without being documented.

By 10 a.m., the situation escalated. Waverly walked past a mural without stopping to pose. She drank a coffee from a regular mug instead of a branded tumbler. She looked at a sunset without considering its aspect ratio. At one point, she encountered a golden retriever on the sidewalk and did not take a single photo of it. "That's when I knew we were in crisis territory," Funnel said. "She loves dogs. She has never NOT photographed a dog. The dog looked confused. I was confused. Everyone was confused."

"She looked at a sunset without considering its aspect ratio. She encountered a golden retriever and didn't photograph it. That's when we knew it was serious."

"She called me around noon, very distressed," Funnel told reporters. "She said she'd been in a park for 45 minutes and hadn't taken a single photo. She didn't know what she was supposed to do with her hands. She said her hands felt 'empty and purposeless.' I said, 'That's how hands normally feel.' She said, 'That can't be right.' I didn't have a response to that."

The Carbonara Incident

The crisis point came at approximately 6:30 p.m. when Waverly attempted to eat dinner at a restaurant without photographing, filming, reviewing, tagging, or geolocating her meal. Witnesses report that she stared at her pasta for seven minutes, picked up her fork, put it down, picked it up again, rotated her plate 15 degrees to the left out of pure muscle memory, realized what she was doing, and then collapsed face-first into her carbonara.

"It was the bravest and most terrifying thing I've ever seen," said fellow diner Marcus Webb, 41, who was seated at an adjacent table. "She just... ate. With no audience. Like an animal. Like all of us. And it broke her."

Emergency services were called at 6:43 p.m. The 911 dispatcher, who has since given a podcast interview about the experience, reported that Funnel was "screaming something about engagement metrics" and that there was "what sounded like carbonara-related distress" in the background.

Medical Assessment: "We Had to Invent a Diagnosis"

Dr. Priya Nandakumar, the attending physician at Cedars-Sinai, said Waverly presented with symptoms so unprecedented that the medical team had to create an entirely new diagnostic category in real time.

"The patient exhibited elevated cortisol levels, disorientation, and a complete inability to determine her own emotional state without first polling an audience," Dr. Nandakumar said during a press briefing. "When we asked how she was feeling, she said she needed to see the engagement numbers first. When we told her there were no engagement numbers, she asked us to 'like and subscribe.' We are medical professionals. We did not know what to do with that."

Dr. Gerald Hutchins, Chief of the Department of Extremely Online Pathologies at UCLA Medical Center (a department that did not exist three years ago and now has a waitlist), was brought in as a consultant. "Catastrophic Authenticity Disorder is a condition in which the patient's sense of self has become so entangled with digital mediation that removal of filters triggers a complete identity collapse," Dr. Hutchins explained. "It's like pulling a tablecloth out from under a place setting, except the place setting is her entire personality and the tablecloth is Instagram."

Waverly was treated with what Dr. Nandakumar described as "a carefully calibrated reintroduction of filtered reality." This involved slowly increasing her screen time in 15-minute intervals, beginning with a Valencia filter on a stock photo of a latte and gradually working up to a full TikTok scroll. By midnight, she was stable enough to apply a subtle Clarendon filter to her hospital bracelet and post it with the caption "healing journey." Her vitals normalized within minutes.

Editor's Note: Cedars-Sinai has asked us to clarify that "Catastrophic Authenticity Disorder" is not yet an official medical diagnosis, that the hospital's Wi-Fi password is not "TouchGrass2026," and that they have banned selfie sticks in the ICU after three separate incidents that they "do not wish to discuss further."

Her Therapist Quit. Then Started an Instagram Account.

In perhaps the most telling detail of the entire saga, Waverly's therapist of three years, Dr. Michelle Tanaka, resigned mid-session during a follow-up appointment on Friday morning.

"I was trying to walk her through a grounding exercise," Dr. Tanaka told News Tabloid Now, speaking from what appeared to be a newly constructed ring-light setup in her living room. "I said, 'Name five things you can see.' She said, 'A wall, a chair, a plant, my reflection, and approximately 2.3 million people who depend on me for content.' I realized at that moment that I was not equipped for this. No one is equipped for this. The DSM is not equipped for this."

Dr. Tanaka has since started her own Instagram account, @DrTanakaTalks, where she posts mental health tips overlaid on aesthetically arranged houseplants. She has 47,000 followers. When asked if she saw the irony, she said, "I see it. I don't care. The engagement is incredible."

"Her followers launched a GoFundMe for 'filter research.' It raised $340,000 in six hours. The money has not been allocated to anything."

The Internet Responds with Maximum Internet

Waverly's followers launched a GoFundMe within hours of the hospitalization titled "Help Brexley: Fund Filter Research So No One Has To See Themselves Again." It raised $340,000 in six hours. The money has not been allocated to anything specific. The campaign description reads, in its entirety: "No one should have to live unfiltered. This is a human rights issue."

A support group has also formed on Reddit called "Faces Anonymous" for people who have accidentally seen their real face without a filter. The group has 12,000 members and a strict no-photos policy. Their tagline is: "We are more than our pores."

The Raw Challenge has since gone viral, with the hashtag #LiveUnfiltered gaining over 4 million uses on social media. Ironically, approximately 99.7% of posts using the hashtag include at least one filter. The remaining 0.3% were posted by users over the age of 65 who do not know how to apply filters and are not aware they are participating in a trend. One of those users is a grandmother in Toledo, Ohio, who posted an unfiltered photo of a casserole. It has 2.1 million likes. She does not know this.

The Recovery: Brave, Unfiltered, and Absolutely Filtered

Waverly was released from the hospital Friday afternoon in what her PR team described as "stable and perfectly content" condition. She immediately posted a 12-part Instagram Story about the experience, which included a brand partnership with a therapy app, a discount code for an "authenticity crystal" that retails for $89, and a sponsored mention of the Apple iPhone 47, which she described as "the perfect tool for my healing journey" -- a statement that is remarkable given that the iPhone 47, as News Tabloid Now reported yesterday, is literally just a mirror.

Her follower count has increased by 340,000 since the incident. She has signed a development deal with Netflix for a docuseries titled "Unfiltered," in which she will attempt to live without filters again, this time with a full production crew, professional lighting, and a post-production team. When asked if this somewhat defeated the purpose, Waverly responded: "I think you're missing the point. The filter was inside me all along."

Her manager nodded. A producer wrote it down. Dr. Tanaka, watching from her new Instagram studio, screen-recorded the moment and posted it with the caption "growth." Somewhere, a journalism professor quietly retired.

As of press time, Waverly has posted 47 stories about her "brave unfiltered journey." Every single one has at least one filter applied. Fourteen of them have more than five. Her most popular post -- a selfie captioned "the real me, no filter" -- uses the Paris filter at 80% opacity, a subtle face-smoothing effect, and what appears to be a $200 preset pack from a photographer in Tulum. It has 1.4 million likes. We are all complicit.
