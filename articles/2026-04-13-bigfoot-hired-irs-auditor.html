Bigfoot Hired by IRS; Reportedly 'The Most Efficient Auditor They've Ever Had'

The legendary cryptid was discovered filing his own taxes at a public library in Washington state, recruited through a non-traditional pipeline, and has since cleared a 47,000-return backlog. His coworkers say he smells like pine needles. They consider this an improvement.

By DIRK SCUTTLEBUTT April 13, 2026 | 5 min read
SHARE:

WASHINGTON, D.C. -- The Internal Revenue Service has confirmed the hiring of a Bigfoot -- specifically, an 8-foot, 600-pound bipedal cryptid who goes by "Hank" -- as a full-time auditor in its Individual Processing Division. Hank, who was recruited through what the IRS described as a "non-traditional talent pipeline," has been employed since January and has already cleared a backlog of 47,000 tax returns, earned a nomination for Employee of the Quarter, and been described by his supervisor as "the best hire I've made in 30 years, and I am including humans in that assessment."

The IRS discovered Hank in October 2025, when a librarian at the Skamania County Public Library in Stevenson, Washington, reported that a "very large, very hairy individual" had been using the library's computers every Tuesday and Thursday for approximately three months. "He always came in around 10 AM, used computer number 7, and left at exactly 2 PM," said librarian Connie Driscoll. "He was very polite. He used the hand sanitizer. He never talked on the phone. Honestly, he was the best patron we had."

Upon investigation by a curious IRS field agent who happened to be in the area for an unrelated audit, it was discovered that Hank was filing his own tax returns. Not one return. Returns going back to 1987. "He had 38 years of unfiled returns, and he was methodically working through them," said Agent Patricia Greenwald, who was the first IRS employee to make contact. "His record-keeping was immaculate. He had receipts. He had categorized deductions. He had a spreadsheet. The spreadsheet was better than mine."

"He smells like pine needles and moss. Honestly, it's better than Greg from accounting. Greg smells like microwave fish and regret. We have asked Greg to stop. Greg will not stop."

When asked what income Hank had been earning that required tax filing, Agent Greenwald paused for a long time before saying: "He appears to have been making a modest living selling artisanal wood carvings at farmers' markets in the Pacific Northwest under the business name 'Hank's Handcrafted Woodland Art.' He has a tax ID number. He has a business license. He has been operating legally this entire time. The only unusual thing is that he is Bigfoot."

The recruitment process was, by IRS standards, remarkably fast. Agent Greenwald, recognizing Hank's aptitude for tax code, offered him a position on the spot. Hank accepted with a nod. He has not spoken a word to anyone at the IRS -- or, to anyone's knowledge, ever. He communicates exclusively through typed messages, handwritten notes, and what coworkers describe as "a series of grunts that somehow convey very specific tax guidance."

Life at the Office

Hank commutes to the IRS processing center in Ogden, Utah, from what his employment file lists as "the forest." He has declined to provide a more specific address, and HR has not pressed the issue, primarily because HR is afraid of him. "Afraid is a strong word," said HR Director Lisa Montoya. "Respectfully cautious. He is eight feet tall. He once accidentally ripped a door off its hinges when he sneezed. We replaced the door. We did not bring it up."

Despite the commute -- the details of which remain unclear, as no one has ever seen Hank arrive or depart, he is simply there when the office opens and gone when it closes -- Hank has never been late. Not once. Not even during the February snowstorm that shut down half of Utah. "Everyone else called in," said his supervisor, Division Chief Robert Tannery. "Hank was at his desk at 7:45 AM, same as always, with snow on his shoulders and a thermos of something that smelled like bark tea. I did not ask about the bark tea. I have learned not to ask."

His typing speed has been clocked at 142 words per minute, a figure that has baffled the IT department given that his fingers are, in the words of one technician, "the size of bratwursts." "I watched him type for 20 minutes once," said IT specialist Derek Nunes. "His hands are enormous. Each finger covers like three keys. And yet, zero typos. Not one. I make typos and my fingers are normal-sized. I have been doing this for 14 years. This creature has been doing it for four months and he is better than me. I am going to be honest with you, it has affected my self-esteem."

"We spent decades proving Bigfoot exists. Decades. Exposed film, plaster casts, thermal imaging, congressional testimony. And the GOVERNMENT had him the whole time? Doing TAXES? I need to sit down."

Hank brings his own lunch every day: a large cooler containing raw salmon, mixed berries, and what appears to be a salad made of ferns. He eats alone in the break room, which his colleagues have come to accept. "At first people were nervous," said auditor Sandra Wilkes. "But then you realize, he is just a guy eating lunch. A very large, very hairy guy eating raw salmon in a government break room. But still. Just a guy. He washes his hands after. He wipes down the table. He is more considerate than most people here."

The Numbers

Hank's productivity has been nothing short of extraordinary. In his first month, he cleared 47,000 backlogged returns -- a figure that would normally take a team of 12 auditors approximately five months. His accuracy rate is 99.97%. The 0.03% error rate consists of a single return in which he transposed two digits on a Social Security number, an error he caught himself four minutes later and corrected with a handwritten note that read "MISTAKE. FIXED. SORRY." in letters that were, given the size of his hands, remarkably neat.

He processes an estimated 340 returns per day. The previous record at the Ogden processing center was 87, held by a woman named Janet who retired in 2019 and whose photograph still hangs in the break room. "Janet was a legend," said Tannery. "Hank processes four Janets a day. I mean that with the deepest respect to Janet. But Hank is operating on a different level. A different, hairier level."

His expertise in tax code is, by all accounts, encyclopedic. During a training session in February, a senior instructor posed a hypothetical question about overlapping Schedule C deductions for a freelance contractor with international income. Hank typed his answer in 11 seconds. The instructor spent 20 minutes verifying it. It was correct. "I have been teaching tax code for 22 years," the instructor said. "I have never seen anyone answer that fast. I have never seen anyone answer that correctly. I have also never seen anyone answer while eating a raw trout, but that is beside the point."

The Conspiracy Community Reacts

The cryptozoology community has responded to the news with a mixture of vindication and fury. Dale Buckner, president of the North American Bigfoot Research Organization, held a press conference outside the IRS building that lasted approximately 45 minutes and consisted primarily of Buckner yelling.

"We spent decades trying to prove Bigfoot exists," Buckner said, his voice cracking. "We hiked through forests. We set up cameras. We analyzed footprints. We testified before Congress. We were laughed at. We were mocked. And now we find out the federal government has known about him the entire time? They didn't just know about him -- they EMPLOYED him? He has a desk? He has a COMPUTER? He's been doing SPREADSHEETS?"

Buckner then sat down on the curb, put his head in his hands, and was heard muttering: "Excel. He uses Excel. Forty years of my life and he uses Excel."

The IRS has declined to address the conspiracy allegations directly but issued a statement that read, in its entirety: "The IRS is an equal opportunity employer. We do not comment on the species of our employees. We are proud of our diverse workforce. Please file your taxes on time."

Employee of the Quarter

Hank has been nominated for Employee of the Quarter by his peers, an honor that comes with a certificate, a $50 gift card to a restaurant of the recipient's choosing, and a reserved parking spot. Hank does not appear to own a car. He has not indicated a restaurant preference. "We offered him the gift card options and he just stared at us," said Montoya. "Then he wrote on a Post-it note: 'I do not require this. Give it to Derek.' Derek is the IT guy. Derek cried. It was a whole thing."

His desk, located in the corner of the open-plan office in a space that was previously used for storage because "no standard desk could accommodate him," is notably bare. There is one personal item: a framed photograph of what appears to be the Loch Ness Monster. When asked about the photograph, Hank typed a single word: "Friend." No further information was provided. No further information was sought.

The IRS union, NTEU Chapter 67, initially filed a grievance over Hank's hiring, citing concerns about "the precedent of hiring non-human entities." The grievance was withdrawn after Hank's first-month performance review. Union representative Michael Torrance explained: "We looked at his numbers. We looked at our numbers. We withdrew the grievance. Hank is making us all look bad, but he is making the agency look good. We have decided to focus on the positive."

As of press time, Hank has begun training two new IRS employees. He communicates instructions through typed documents that his trainees describe as "thorough, well-organized, and only slightly terrifying." Both trainees have requested to remain on Hank's team permanently. One described the experience as "the best professional development I've ever received from someone who could throw me through a wall."
