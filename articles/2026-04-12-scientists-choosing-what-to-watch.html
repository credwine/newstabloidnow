Scientists Confirm Average Human Spends More Time Choosing What to Watch Than Actually Watching

A landmark 12-year study reveals the devastating truth about your evening routine: you are spending your life scrolling through thumbnails of shows you will never start.

By REX HEADLINER April 12, 2026 | 4 min read
SHARE:

CAMBRIDGE, MA - A landmark study published this week in the journal Nature Human Behaviour has confirmed what researchers have long suspected and what your partner has been telling you for years: the average human spends significantly more time choosing what to watch on streaming platforms than actually watching anything.

The study, conducted over 12 years by a team at MIT's Department of Media Indecision (established specifically for this research), tracked the viewing habits of 14,000 participants across six continents. The findings are, in the researchers' own words, "profoundly depressing."

"Our data shows that the average adult spends 1 hour and 47 minutes per day scrolling through streaming platform interfaces," said lead researcher Dr. Anita Pham, presenting the findings at a press conference she had trouble deciding whether to hold in person or over Zoom. "They spend only 1 hour and 12 minutes actually watching content. The remaining 35 minutes are spent telling someone else, 'I don't know, what do you want to watch?'"

The Numbers Are Staggering

According to the study's data, which has been peer-reviewed by scientists who admitted they spent 40 minutes deciding which journal to submit it to, the situation is far worse than previously estimated:

  • The average person scrolls past 73 titles per browsing session before selecting one
  • 38% of the time, the selected title is abandoned within 6 minutes in favor of "something else"
  • "Something else" is scrolled for an additional 22 minutes before the user falls asleep
  • Over a lifetime, the average person will spend 2.4 years scrolling through streaming menus
  • This is longer than the average person spends exercising (1.3 years), cooking (2.1 years), or being happy (disputed)
  • 67% of participants reported adding shows to their watchlist that they have no intention of ever watching

Perhaps most alarmingly, the study found that couples spend an average of 32 minutes negotiating what to watch together, a process the researchers categorized as "low-stakes diplomacy with catastrophically high emotional investment."

"We've essentially created a generation of people who are more comfortable browsing for entertainment than experiencing it."

The Streaming Platforms Respond

Netflix, which was provided with an advance copy of the study, has responded by announcing a new premium feature called "Just Pick Something," available for $4.99 per month on top of existing subscription fees. The feature will automatically select a show based on the user's viewing history, time of day, and "general vibe," a metric Netflix declined to elaborate on.

"We recognize that choice is the enemy of contentment," said Netflix spokesperson Rachel Torres. "For nearly $5 more per month, we will eliminate the burden of free will from your evening. You're welcome."

Hulu has countered with a feature called "Watch This or We Cancel It," which gives users 48 hours to watch a recommended show before it is removed from the platform entirely. The feature has been described by Hulu's head of product as "motivational."

Amazon Prime Video has taken a different approach, announcing that it will add 12,000 additional titles to its library, bringing the total to an estimated 4.7 million, on the theory that "if we add enough content, eventually someone will watch something by accident."

Editor's Note: Disney+ did not respond to our request for comment, as their media relations team was reportedly stuck in a meeting trying to decide which press release template to use.

The Psychological Impact

Dr. Leonard Marsh, a behavioral psychologist at Stanford who was not involved in the study but has been thinking about watching a documentary about it, said the findings reflect a broader crisis of decision-making in modern society.

"What we're seeing is analysis paralysis on a civilizational scale," Dr. Marsh told News Tabloid Now from his living room, where a Netflix homepage was visible on the television behind him, paused on the browse screen. "The paradox of choice is real. When you have 50,000 options, the rational response is to choose none of them and go to bed angry."

The study also identified a new psychological phenomenon the researchers have dubbed "The Scroll of Shame," defined as the moment a user realizes they have been browsing for 45 minutes and decides to watch a show they have already seen three times because "at least I know I like it."

According to the data, The Office accounts for 23% of all "Scroll of Shame" selections in the United States. In the UK, this figure is held by Taskmaster. In Japan, it is a cooking show about a man who eats dinner alone, which researchers noted was "on the nose."

What Can Be Done

The researchers offered several recommendations for reducing scroll time, none of which they believe will be followed:

  1. Set a 5-minute browsing timer. When it goes off, watch whatever your cursor is on.
  2. Close your eyes and select randomly. This is essentially what you're doing anyway, but faster.
  3. Read a book. (This recommendation was included "for completeness" and the researchers acknowledged it was "unrealistic.")
  4. Cancel all streaming subscriptions and stare at a wall, which the study found provides a comparable level of satisfaction.

Dr. Pham concluded the press conference by noting that the study itself took 12 years partly because the research team spent the first 18 months deciding which statistical software to use.

"We see the irony," she said. "We don't find it funny."
