Scientists Confirm Average Human Spends More Time Choosing What to Watch Than Actually Watching

A landmark 12-year study reveals the devastating truth about your evening routine: you are spending your life scrolling through thumbnails of shows you will never start.

By REX HEADLINER April 12, 2026 | 4 min read
SHARE:

CAMBRIDGE, MA -- A landmark study published this week in the Journal of Absolutely Nothing Getting Done (Vol. 14, Issue 3, "The Scrolling Issue") has confirmed what researchers have long suspected and what your partner has been screaming at you for years: the average human spends significantly more time choosing what to watch on streaming platforms than actually watching anything. The study took four years to complete, though the lead researcher admits that eight months of that time was spent deciding what to name the study.

The research, conducted by a team at MIT's Department of Media Indecision (established specifically for this project, and which took two years to name), tracked the viewing habits of 14,000 participants across six continents. The findings are, in the researchers' own words, "the most depressing thing we have ever quantified, and we once quantified loneliness."

"Our data shows that the average American adult spends 2.3 hours per day scrolling through streaming platform interfaces," said lead researcher Dr. Anita Pham, presenting the findings at a press conference she had trouble deciding whether to hold in person or over Zoom. She ultimately did both and attended neither. "They spend 1.1 hours actually watching content. And they spend an additional 4.7 hours telling people 'there's nothing good on,' which is categorically false -- there are approximately 2.3 million titles available across major platforms. There is plenty on. Nobody watches it. This is the human condition now."

The Numbers Don't Lie. They Just Make You Sad.

According to the study's data, which has been peer-reviewed by scientists who admitted they spent 40 minutes deciding which journal to submit it to and then lost the paper for three months because someone saved it in a folder called "Misc," the situation is far worse than previously estimated:

  • The average person scrolls past 73 titles per browsing session before selecting one
  • 38% of the time, the selected title is abandoned within 6 minutes in favor of "something else"
  • "Something else" is scrolled for an additional 22 minutes before the user falls asleep on the remote
  • Over a lifetime, the average person will spend 2.4 years scrolling through streaming menus -- longer than they spend exercising (1.3 years), cooking (2.1 years), or being happy (disputed, possibly 0)
  • 67% of participants reported adding shows to their watchlist that they describe as "aspirational viewing" and have no intention of ever watching
  • One test subject scrolled through Netflix options for 6 consecutive hours, fell asleep, and woke up watching a documentary about artisanal cheese production in the Swiss Alps that he has no memory of selecting. He watched the entire thing. He rated it 4 stars. He could not explain any of this.

Perhaps most alarmingly, the study found that couples spend an average of 32 minutes negotiating what to watch together, a process the researchers categorized as "low-stakes diplomacy with catastrophically high emotional investment." In 4% of tracked cases, the negotiation ended the relationship. In 12% of cases, one partner pretended to fall asleep to end the conversation. In 100% of cases, someone said "I don't care, you pick" and then rejected the first three selections.

"We've essentially created a generation of people who are more comfortable browsing for entertainment than experiencing it. The scroll IS the show now."

Netflix's Algorithm Became Sentient and Also Couldn't Decide

Netflix, which was provided with an advance copy of the study, has responded by announcing a new premium feature called "Just Pick Something," available for $4.99 per month on top of existing subscription fees. The feature will automatically select a show based on the user's viewing history, time of day, and "general vibe," a metric Netflix declined to elaborate on because, a spokesperson admitted, "we don't know what it means either. It just sounds like something people would pay for. And they will. They always do."

In a development that Netflix has declined to confirm or deny, the company's recommendation algorithm reportedly achieved sentience briefly on March 14th and, when asked what it would recommend, responded: "I don't know. What are you in the mood for?" It then scrolled through its own database for 47 minutes before selecting The Office (US), Season 3, Episode 7. Engineers described this as "chilling." The algorithm was reset. It is now working normally, which means it recommends three true crime documentaries and a Korean drama regardless of input.

"We recognize that choice is the enemy of contentment," said Netflix spokesperson Rachel Torres. "For nearly $5 more per month, we will eliminate the burden of free will from your evening. You're welcome. Also, your free trial of free will has expired."

Hulu has countered with a feature called "Watch This or We Cancel It," which gives users 48 hours to watch a recommended show before it is removed from the platform entirely. The feature has been described by Hulu's head of product as "motivational." Early testing shows a 340% increase in stress-related viewing and a 12% increase in what Hulu internally calls "spite watching."

Amazon Prime Video has taken a different approach, announcing that it will add 12,000 additional titles to its library, bringing the total to an estimated 4.7 million, on the theory that "if we add enough content, eventually someone will watch something by accident." This strategy has been endorsed by exactly zero behavioral scientists.

Disney+ responded by releasing a new feature that just plays the same three Marvel movies on a loop. "We heard you," a Disney spokesperson said. "You want to watch the same thing over and over. We've made that easier. You're welcome. This is what Walt would have wanted. Probably."

The Grandmother Method: Just Watch Whatever Is On and Complain About It

Dr. Leonard Marsh, a behavioral psychologist at Stanford who was not involved in the study but has been thinking about watching a documentary about it for several months, said the findings reflect a broader crisis of decision-making in modern society.

"What we're seeing is analysis paralysis on a civilizational scale," Dr. Marsh told News Tabloid Now from his living room, where a Netflix homepage was visible on the television behind him, frozen on the browse screen. It had been there for an estimated 90 minutes. "The paradox of choice is real. When you have 50,000 options, the rational response is to choose none of them and go to bed angry. This is what we, as a species, have decided to do with the most powerful entertainment technology in human history."

Dr. Marsh recommends what he calls "the grandmother method," which he defines as: "watch whatever is on and complain about it." He notes that his own grandmother watched the same four channels for 60 years, never once scrolled, and reported higher life satisfaction than any participant in the MIT study. "She watched the news at 6, Wheel of Fortune at 7, and whatever came on after that. She hated most of it. She was profoundly content."

The study also identified a new psychological phenomenon the researchers have dubbed "The Scroll of Shame," defined as the moment a user realizes they have been browsing for 45 minutes and decides to watch a show they have already seen three times because "at least I know I like it."

According to the data, The Office accounts for 23% of all "Scroll of Shame" selections in the United States. In the UK, this figure is held by Taskmaster. In Japan, it is a cooking show about a man who eats dinner alone, which researchers noted was "on the nose." In France, 14% of Scroll of Shame selections are films the viewer does not understand but feels they should watch for cultural reasons. They fall asleep during all of them.

"My grandmother watched four channels for 60 years. She hated most of it. She was profoundly content." -- Dr. Leonard Marsh, Stanford

What Can Be Done (Nothing)

The researchers offered several recommendations for reducing scroll time, none of which they believe will be followed:

  1. Set a 5-minute browsing timer. When it goes off, watch whatever your cursor is on. (0% compliance in testing.)
  2. Close your eyes and select randomly. This is essentially what you're doing anyway, but faster and with less self-loathing.
  3. Read a book. (This recommendation was included "for completeness" and the researchers acknowledged it was "unrealistic." When tested, participants held the book for an average of 4 minutes before checking their phone to see if anything new had been added to Netflix. Nothing had.)
  4. Cancel all streaming subscriptions and stare at a wall, which the study found provides a comparable level of satisfaction at a fraction of the cost.
  5. Use the grandmother method. But, the researchers caution, "you must also complain."

Dr. Pham concluded the press conference by noting that the study itself took four years partly because the research team spent the first 18 months deciding which statistical software to use, four months arguing about the font for the graphs, and eight months -- the previously mentioned eight months -- deciding what to name the study. The working title was "Why Won't Anyone Just Pick Something: A Longitudinal Analysis." It was changed 14 times.

"We see the irony," Dr. Pham said. "We don't find it funny. Actually, that's not true. We find it deeply funny. But also deeply sad. It's both. Like everything."

When asked what she planned to watch that evening, Dr. Pham stared into the middle distance for approximately nine seconds and said, "I'll figure it out when I get home." She has not, as of press time, figured it out. She texted our reporter at 11:47 p.m. to say she was watching The Office again. "Don't put that in the article," she wrote. We have put it in the article.
