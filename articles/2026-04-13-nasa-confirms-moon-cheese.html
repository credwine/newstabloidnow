NASA Confirms Moon Is 34% Cheese; Admits They've 'Known Since 1972'

In a press conference that NASA described as "long overdue," scientists revealed that lunar samples collected during Apollo missions contain significant dairy content. Wisconsin has already proposed a harvesting mission. France has filed a complaint with the UN.

By TANYA BLITZ April 13, 2026 | 6 min read
SHARE:

HOUSTON -- In a press conference that lasted 47 minutes and included three apologies, two resignations, and one person fainting, NASA officially confirmed Thursday that the Moon is approximately 34% cheese. The announcement, which the agency described as "long overdue" and scientists described as "the most embarrassing thing that has ever happened to astrophysics," comes after 54 years of what NASA Administrator Dr. James Hargrove called "a series of internal disagreements about how to tell people."

The revelation stems from lunar samples collected during the Apollo 14 mission in 1971. Astronaut Edgar Mitchell brought back a 4.7-kilogram rock from the Fra Mauro highlands that, upon return to the Lunar Receiving Laboratory in Houston, "smelled funny." The sample was cataloged, sealed in a nitrogen-filled container, and placed on a shelf in Building 31, where it remained undisturbed for 27 years.

In 1998, a janitor named Carl Wendt -- who has since been described by NASA as "an unauthorized sample tester" and by Carl himself as "a guy who was hungry and curious" -- opened the container during a night shift and ate a small piece of the sample. According to the incident report, which was classified until Thursday, Wendt told his supervisor the next morning that the sample "tasted like gouda." His supervisor, Dr. Helen Marchetti, reportedly laughed, then stopped laughing, then ran several tests, then stopped sleeping for approximately two weeks.

"We thought it was a contamination issue. We tested for contamination for six years. It was not contamination. The Moon is just made of cheese. I need everyone to understand how difficult that sentence was for me to say out loud."

Extensive testing conducted between 1998 and 2026 by NASA's Astromaterials Research and Exploration Science Division -- a name that, as Dr. Holt noted during the press conference, "sounds much more dignified than what we've been doing, which is mostly arguing about cheese" -- confirmed the following composition of the lunar samples:

  • 34% cheese (primarily resembling aged gouda, with trace compounds consistent with parmesan)
  • 41% basaltic rock (standard lunar geology)
  • 25% an unknown calcium-based compound that, according to Dr. Holt, "tastes like parmesan, which is not the same as being parmesan, but the distinction has become increasingly academic"

Dr. Holt, who has led the research team for the past eight years and who appeared at the press conference with the energy of someone who has been carrying a very heavy secret for a very long time, said: "We thought it was a contamination issue. We tested for contamination for six years. It was not contamination. We then thought it was a labeling error. It was not a labeling error. We then thought maybe Carl just ate some gouda he had in his pocket and got confused. Carl did not have gouda in his pocket. We have verified this extensively. The Moon is just made of cheese. I need everyone to understand how difficult that sentence was for me to say out loud, and I have a PhD from Caltech."

The Dairy Industry Responds

The American dairy industry responded to the announcement with what can only be described as aggressive enthusiasm. Within 72 hours of the press conference, 14 patents had been filed by various dairy corporations, ranging from "Lunar Cheese Extraction Methods" to "Moon-Aged Gouda: A New Premium Product Category" to, most alarmingly, a patent titled simply "Owning the Moon."

Dean Foods issued a statement calling the discovery "the most significant development in dairy since pasteurization" and announced the creation of a new subsidiary called Dean Lunar, which will focus on "extraterrestrial dairy procurement." The subsidiary already has 47 employees and a logo.

Wisconsin Governor Rebecca Walters held an emergency press conference of her own in Madison, calling the news "the best thing this state has received since the invention of cheese curds." She proposed what she called the "Wisconsin Lunar Cheese Harvesting Initiative," a $2.3 billion state-funded mission to "go up there and get some." When reporters pointed out that Wisconsin does not have a space program, Walters said, "We'll build one. We built the cheese industry from scratch. The Moon is just the next logical step." She then unveiled a bumper sticker that read "WISCONSIN: FIRST IN CHEESE, FIRST ON THE MOON" and was escorted offstage by an aide who appeared to be trying very hard not to say something.

"This challenges everything we know about planetary formation. Or everything we know about cheese. Honestly, at this point, I am not sure which would be more unsettling."

International Incident

The announcement has already sparked an international dispute. France filed a formal complaint with the United Nations within four hours of the press conference, arguing that lunar cheese "belongs to no nation, but especially not to Wisconsin." French President Michel Larue called an emergency session of the National Assembly, during which he delivered a 22-minute address that the English-language press has translated as: "The Moon cheese is clearly of French origin. The composition is consistent with a traditional Normandy-style gouda. We demand an international tribunal."

Italy responded within the hour, noting that the 25% parmesan component "is unambiguously Italian in character" and demanded "appropriate recognition and, ideally, royalties." The Italian ambassador to the United Nations, Giulia Ferretti, told reporters: "Parmesan is a protected designation of origin under EU law. If the Moon contains parmesan, then a portion of the Moon is, legally speaking, Italian. We have lawyers looking into this. Many lawyers. Angry lawyers."

Switzerland declined to comment, which several diplomats described as "very Swiss."

The Netherlands issued a brief statement that read: "Gouda is Dutch. The Moon is Dutch. End of discussion." The statement was followed by a second statement, issued 11 minutes later, that read: "We are aware that the first statement may have been premature. We stand by its spirit."

Operation Brie

NASA has announced a return mission to the Moon, code-named "Operation Brie," scheduled for 2028. The mission's stated objective is to collect larger samples from multiple sites to "determine the full extent and variety of the Moon's dairy composition." Privately, several NASA officials have acknowledged that the mission name was chosen "because it was funnier than Operation Cheddar," and that the mission's secondary objective is "to determine if the cheese is edible at scale, which is a sentence I never thought I would include in a mission brief."

The mission will use a modified Artemis lander equipped with what NASA is calling a "Dairy Extraction Module," a refrigerated sample container designed to transport up to 200 kilograms of lunar cheese back to Earth. "We are taking this extremely seriously," said mission director Dr. Priya Subramaniam. "This is a scientific endeavor. The fact that it involves cheese does not make it less scientific. I keep telling people this. They keep laughing. I am getting tired of the laughing."

The European Space Agency has announced a competing mission, code-named "Fromage Lunaire," which France insisted on naming despite ESA headquarters being in Paris, where they could have just asked. Japan's space agency, JAXA, has quietly begun its own program, which it has declined to name but which leaked documents refer to as "Project Moon Bento."

Scientific Community Divided

The scientific community has reacted with a mixture of fascination, confusion, and what one researcher described as "the kind of deep, silent staring that usually precedes a career change."

Dr. Marcus Lehmann, Chair of Astrophysics at MIT, called the discovery "paradigm-shattering." "This challenges everything we know about planetary formation," he said. "The current models of lunar formation -- the giant impact hypothesis, the co-accretion theory -- none of them account for dairy. Not one. I have checked. We are going to have to rewrite the textbooks. Specifically, the parts about the Moon. Which is, you know, a significant part."

Dr. Yuki Tanaka, a planetary scientist at the University of Tokyo, offered a more measured response: "This challenges everything we know about planetary formation. Or everything we know about cheese. Honestly, at this point, I am not sure which would be more unsettling. I have spent my entire career studying the Moon. I now have to consider the possibility that I have been studying cheese. My wife has been very supportive. She also has not stopped laughing since Thursday."

The vegan community issued a joint statement from 14 organizations expressing "profound confusion." The statement, which was three pages long, could be summarized as: "We have questions. Many questions. We are not sure who to direct them to. We have decided to direct them to everyone." PETA announced it would "oppose any harvesting of Moon cheese on ethical grounds," though when pressed on what animal was harmed in the creation of Moon cheese, a spokesperson said, "We are still working through that."

Carl Wendt, the janitor who started it all, was reached by phone at his home in suburban Houston. When asked how he felt about the announcement, he said: "I've been telling people for 28 years that the Moon tastes like gouda. They all thought I was crazy. My ex-wife thought I was crazy. My therapist thought I was crazy. My therapist's therapist, who I should not have been seeing, also thought I was crazy. I am vindicated. I am going to have a grilled cheese tonight. The regular kind. From Earth. But I will eat it with satisfaction."
